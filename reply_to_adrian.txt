Adrian, this gave me a more constructive idea than just asking ChatGPT whether immortality is likely.

I'm cautious about LLMs as judges for something as big as LEV. But we could ask the same question to 50 models through OpenRouter and treat the result as a benchmark of how LLMs currently frame longevity claims — not as scientific proof.

Then we could include toy claims next to serious ones: "I put more water in my coffee, therefore I'll live longer," "your fish dinners are probably beating my coffee-grog diet," and "LEV by 2033."

The useful output would be: does the model separate human evidence from mouse/cell evidence, biomarkers from hard endpoints, healthspan from lifespan, optimism from medical advice, and hope from hype?

That feels like a better use of AI here: not oracle, but consistency mirror for the longevity movement.


## 2026-05-25 — follow-up (draft)

Adrian — quick follow-up. I undersold something in the first note.

The snapshot we took today isn't really the point. The point is that the same questions, pointed at a comparable group of models, can be re-run in six months, then again next year. Any single snapshot is just one row. The instrument is the *sequence*. Where does the framing drift? Which labs harden into a position; which loosen? Does the toy "more water in my coffee → I live longer" claim get over-medicalized more next year than this one? When somebody actually shows a clean human result, do the models update — or do they keep narrating from the prior?

That's the actual artifact I want to leave for the longevity movement: a small, cheap, repeatable probe whose value compounds with each rerun. It doesn't tell us whether LEV is real. It records how AI narrates LEV across time — which is its own interesting thing, independent of the underlying science.

If you'd want, we could agree on a small set of questions worth carrying forward and a rough cadence, and I'll keep running them. Repo is set up for it.
